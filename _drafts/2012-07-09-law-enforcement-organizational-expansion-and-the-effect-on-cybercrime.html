The fear of Cyber War seems to have been a relatively effective catalyst for making a plan for cyber defense in many countries - budget or no budget - and the areas of cyber security and cybercrime investigation are getting a boost in its wake.

As more of the public become aware of some vague looming threat in the cyber world, they demand increased protection. Countries respond to this national need by increasing their national resources, adding additional law enforcement staff, increasing budgets, creating new units, etc. However, as transnational cybercrime increases, and a country increases its capacity to investigate these crimes, capacity expansion will eventually begin to affect the workload other countries.

Take for example a country (Requestor 1) whose investigation capacity allows for 3 international investigation requests over X amount of time (Fig. 1). Now assume Requestor 1 has the resources to double their national cybercrime investigation capacity. After capacity expansion, Requestor 1 is able to double the amount of international requests over X amount of time (Fig. 2).
Fig. 1 - Requestor 1 with investigation capacity that allows for 3 international requests at a time
Fig. 2 - Requestor 1 with investigation capacity increased that now allows for 6 international requests at a time


Consider Country C, who has little investigation capacity and no resources to expand capacity. Many countries have issues keeping up with their national requests. If Country C is barely, or not even, keeping up with their national requests, and Requestor 1 increases the number of requests to Country C, then Country C will be less able to meet both national and international requests (Fig. 3), potentially resulting in a backlog, ignored requests, or reduction of scope of services.
