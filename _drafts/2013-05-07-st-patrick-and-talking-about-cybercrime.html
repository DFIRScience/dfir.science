--- layout: post title: St. Patrick and Talking About Cybercrime date: '2013-05-07T19:05:00.000+09:00' author: Joshua tags: - Education - Discussion modified_time: '2013-05-07T19:05:11.073+09:00' blogger_id: tag:blogger.com,1999:blog-2701259639305045003.post-9099140628412600932 blogger_orig_url: http://www.cybercrimetech.com/2013/05/st-patrick-and-talking-about-cybercrime.html --- Having been in Ireland for a considerable amount of time, I've become well aware of the significance of the shamrock, and its symbolism beyond commercial associations.

The story, in a nutshell, is that St. Patrick used the shamrock to explain the "Holy Trinity". This is a pretty complicated, abstract concept, but by using the shamrock to signify the three bodies as one, it helped people understand and accept the idea.

St. Patrick, in short, had a genius for presentation. His ability to reduce complicated ideas into an easily understood form, that could then in turn be easily explained by new members, helped bring Catholicism to Ireland, and turned a plentiful plant into a symbol of a country's faith.

Fast-forward a few centuries where cybercrime is quite a vague term. Concretely defining cybercrime has proven to be difficult, and what attempts were made usually resulted in different definitions and scope from country to country. Understanding cybercrime - and agreeing on the definition - is difficult even for experts in the field who deal with it on a regular basis. If experts have difficulty in accurately communicating about cybercrime to other experts, how then are they able to explain it to non-experts?

As the general public become more aware of cybercrime, a lack of universal definition is causing confusion, and fear of some giant, looming, unknowable danger. For example, in this article INTERPOL is focusing on 'fighting cybercrime' with brief references to untouchable organized gangs online, political-oriented attacks, and military organizations. All this, and the fact that "cybercrime is becoming more international", but nowhere is cybercrime actually defined in an easy to understand way. Not only is the problem of cybercrime not clear, but what to do about it is also not clear.

As practitioners dealing with cybercrime, we need to find our 'shamrock'. If a common definition can be established, then we can begin to simplify, explain and educate. The more knowledge people (judges, managers, the public) have, the more they can attempt to protect themselves, and society from a now-identifiable threat.
